This family measures how often a model solves a task and how much that result depends on the repair loop. The distinction is essential: a model that solves on the first attempt is different from a model that reaches the same final success rate only after repeated retries.

\begin{table}[H]\small
\centering
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}l l Y Y@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Formula} & \textbf{What it measures} & \textbf{Main interpretation}\\
\midrule
SR & \(\mathrm{mean}(\mathrm{Valid})\) & Fraction of instances solved within the retry budget. & Headline outcome, but insufficient alone.\\
\addlinespace
FASR & \(\mathrm{mean}(\mathrm{Valid}\land \mathrm{Iterations}=1)\) & Fraction solved without feedback. & Strongest signal of zero-shot planning ability.\\
\addlinespace
IWSR & \(\mathrm{mean}(1/k)\) for solved instances at iteration \(k\), else \(0\) & Success discounted by attempts needed. & Rewards fast convergence and penalizes late wins.\\
\addlinespace
Retry Gap & \(\mathrm{SR}-\mathrm{FASR}\) & Share of success attributable to retries. & Large gap means SR is retry-inflated.\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\caption{Planning-efficiency metrics.}
\label{tab:efficiency-metrics-compact}
\end{table}

The three success metrics obey \(\mathrm{FASR}\leq\mathrm{IWSR}\leq\mathrm{SR}\). If all three are close, the model succeeds early and the repair loop adds little. If SR is high but FASR is low, the model depends heavily on retries. The full implementation details are in Appendix~\ref{app:extended-metric-families}.
